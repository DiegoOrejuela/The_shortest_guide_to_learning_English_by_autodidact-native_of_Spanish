Grammar

Grammar will be the most appropriate language component to start learning a language in an adult age, as explained at the beginning of this blog. We will review its definition and will delve into its two major areas: morphology and syntactic.

Definition

Grammar is the group of principles, rules and precepts that govern the use of a particular language. It treats its elementary components and the combinations that exist between them. In simpler words, it deals with the structure and rules of the relationship between signs. Therefore, each language usually has its own grammar.

Components

Because the part of the language it deals with is too broad, a thematic division has been identified in two areas:

Morphology

from the Greek morphe (form) and logia (treaty or study)

It addresses the internal structure of words and their training process. The word is the minimum unit of meaning of a language, and two parts can be found in it:

  • Lexeme

basic and invariable morph. It is considered its root.

  • Gramema

Morph that constitutes the variable part.

Syntactic

from Greek without (with), taxis (order) and -tico (relative to)

His focus is on the order and combination of words to form sentences and phrases that allow two entities to send messages. Let's see an example to understand it better:

go Carol doesn't the cinema to

Carol doesn't go to the cinema.

In the first statement we know the meanings of each word used, but we can not get any message. In the second, due to the order and the correct combination of the words, we can deduce a message.

To achieve construct messages, the syntactic classifies the words according to their function:

  • Noun or substantive

from Latin substantia (essence)

Name people, living beings, things, events, places or abstract concepts.

Maria
Dog
Tables
Christmas
London
Love

  • Article

from the Latin Articulus (joint, member)

The article is the variable part of the sentence that always accompanies a noun, to express mainly its gender, its number and its grammatical person.

the
a
an

  • Adjective

from the Latin ad- (next to, to), the verb iacere (assign) and the suffix -tivo (indicates active or passive relationship)

Assign or mark assignments to the noun. Qualifies or determines the properties and characteristics of the noun, pronouns and even other adjectives.

Ugly
Tall
Nice

  • Pronoun

from Latin pronomen, formed from the prefix pro- (in place of) and nomen (name)

They replace "or" are equivalent "to a name or noun, when this is already known or has been indicated, in most cases.

  • Verb

from Latin verbum (word) and from the Indo-European root weriga (call, summon)

The word that calls or summons something. Indicates action, process or state that the subject performs or suffers.

go
Love
Lost
Play

  • Adverb

from the Latin adverbium, formed from the prefix ad- (together, towards) and verbum.

The main function of adverbs is to act as modifiers or complements of a verb. It gives information about the way, place, time, certainty, or other circumstances of the activity expressed by the verb. You can also modify adverbs or adjectives.

Often
Much
Forever
Surely


  • Preposition

from the Latin praepositio, formed by prae- (front, before) and positio (position)

Unchanging part of the sentence that unites words indicating the relationship they have with each other. Indicates origin, origin, destination, place, address, etc.

To
Since
of
Between


  • Conjunction

From Latin cum (with) and jungo (join)

Words that put together or unite. They function as a link between words and sentences, and how they have no meaning.

Then
After
Therefore
so

  • Interjection

from the Latin interiectio, conformed by the prefix inter- (between, at intervals), the verb iacere (throw, throw, see)

Independent word within a sentence that expresses feelings or strong moods.

Hey!
Ouch!
Booo!
Pst!
